Overview

Reading books can be very relaxing and enjoyable - a perfect antidote for a hectic lifestyle. Unfortunately, for someone with a 9 to 5 job, setting aside time and getting into the mood can be difficult. Books can get repetitive, boring and hard to read. You might forget where you’d left off, or not remember what you’ve read so far. Ditti is an iPad app designed to make reading easier.

The Challenge

Make reading easier. Help readers find what's important to them.

The Solution

Ditti is your reading buddy on a tab. To make reading easier and relevant, Ditti has three main features.

  1. Twitter recommendations - Ditti finds book recommendations from the people you follow on twitter.
  2. Highlights - Ditti lets you pick recommenders from twitter and view their highlights. If a friend recommends a book, you can view their highlights. This makes it easier to read - you can read the relevant parts of the book and skip the unnecessary details.
  3. Recap - A summary of what you’ve read so far.
Ditti also keeps track of the books you’ve read so far, provides reading stats and reads the book out to you.

Research

I interviewed 5 people aged 20-40 to understand their needs and pain-points when it comes to reading.

“Off-late, especially non-fiction, I use twitter recommendations. My twitter list is my list of influences. I want an easy way to get a list of recommendations from my twitter list. I want a list of books to read from all the people I follow”

The genre has shifted from fiction to reading non-fiction, self-help books. These books are usually recommendations from friends, colleagues or twitter feeds.

“I have about 15-20 mins to read - give me a 15 min version of the book with key takeaways”
“I feel like I don’t have the time to read.”

Reading Non-fiction can be difficult. They are often dry, repetitive and boring. Readers would like to consume relevant content, quickly.

“I’m halfway through reading ‘hey whipple squeeze this’ - I don’t remember where I left off, or I’ve forgotten what I’ve read and lost the track. I spend time flipping back and re-reading”

Forgetting what’s been read so far can be frustrating. Re-reading the books takes much more effort.

“I love the feel of a new book, It’s really nice when I have a physical thing in my hand”

Most participants prefer reading physical books compared to kindles. They love the feel of holding a book and flipping through each page.

Defining the user

Kiran is an avid reader with a busy work schedule. He reads books primarily to learn, grow, and feed his curiosities. This means that he mostly reads non-fiction books about personal development, healthy lifestyle, and business/marketing. He buys books based on recommendations from friends, colleagues and twitter. Non-fiction can get repetitive and hard to read. Kiran's goal is to read parts of the book that are relevant to him, in the little time that he gets to read. He finds it frustrating to remember where he left off, flipping back re-reading parts he’s forgotten. He wants to quickly get to the interesting/relevant bits and skip the rest.

Ideation

To define the problem better, I created Point of View statements along with How Might We questions based on the insights I discovered through research and picked 3 Focus PoVs

I used the crazy-8s method to quickly ideate on my focus PoVs. I sketched 8 ideas each in 8 minutes. I repeated this process and worked on refining my favorites.

I sketched out a storyboard, to illustrate How ditti would help Kiran read more efficiently

Strategy

I created a microgram user flow based on my persona Kiran and the choices he’d make while navigating Ditti.

I refined the rough sketches from the crazy-8s method into mid-fidelity wireframes.

Interaction Design

The brand is relaxed and clean with focus on the content, making sure readers aren’t distracted by the colors. The color palette is balanced by the clean typography. I created a logo that was cheerful and friendly.

UI Designs

Ditti remembers the books you’ve read and your book marks. For each unfinished book, Ditti provides a little snippet from where you left off to inspire you to read further. Ditti can also discuss the book with you.

Reader specific statistics are used to encourage making reading a habit.

Ditti curates books recommended by the people you follow on Twitter. Each book has a list of twitter-reviews from your feed. Interested readers can click on the book to buy it on amazon.

Add New Book

The reader can scan the Ditti QR code at the back of the book. Ditti recognises the book and fetches the e-reader version, along with recommender details

Highlights

Ditti provides personalized skimming through highlights. Recommenders can highlight what they like in the book and the reader can choose to see these highlights.

Recap

Ditti’s bookmarks include summaries. If you don’t quite remember what you’ve read so far, Ditti gives you a recap.

Usability Testing

I conducted a usability test across 7 participants that were similar to my persona. The overall feedback was extremely positive. Favorite features were recap and twitter recommendations.

“I love the recaps, sign me up for the release!”

Based on the test results, I created an affinity map to better understand how I could improve Ditti. I analyzed my test results and organised them into four categories.

Reflections and Next Steps

This has been one of the more rewarding projects I’ve worked on. I truly enjoyed watching Ditti evolve and loved by my test participants. Although it’s tempting to design for all of the user pain points I discovered, Prioritising Point of Views helped me focus on a single problem. Crazy-8s is a fantastic technique to help brainstorm ideas.

I would like to explore how Ditti could retain the ‘feel’ of holding and reading a physical book. Perhaps it gets lighter as you read more.
